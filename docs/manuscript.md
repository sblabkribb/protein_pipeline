# protein\_pipeline: An End-to-End Automated Pipeline for Protein Design and Evaluation

## Abstract

Computational protein design workflows commonly span heterogeneous tools for sequence retrieval, backbone generation, sequence design, structure prediction, filtering, and downstream analysis, yet these stages are often connected through ad hoc scripts that hinder reproducibility and comparative interpretation [8]. We present `protein_pipeline`, an end-to-end system for protein design workflow orchestration and interactive analysis that integrates a Model Context Protocol (MCP)-enabled backend with a multi-agent consensus panel for autonomous quality control. Unlike traditional linear pipelines, `protein_pipeline` implements scientific guardrails that monitor stage-level quality signals—such as MSA depth (threshold < 50), ProteinMPNN sampling statistics, SoluProt solubility pass rates (threshold < 20%), and AlphaFold2 pLDDT confidence scores (threshold < 75)—to automate "proceed", "monitor", or "recover" decisions across iterative design rounds. By coupling MCP-enabled tool execution with real-time agentic interpretation and structured artifact management under persistent `run_id` directories, the platform reframes protein design from a collection of disconnected jobs into a traceable, self-correcting research workflow. This work demonstrates how agentic systems can bridge the operational gap in AI-assisted protein engineering, emphasizing reproducible scientific workflow design over new predictive model architectures [1, 13].

## Introduction

Computational protein design workflows increasingly combine sequence retrieval, multiple sequence alignment, backbone generation or selection, sequence design, developability filtering, structure prediction, and downstream novelty or comparative analysis. Although individual components may be supported by mature models or services, practical end-to-end use is often distributed across scripts, notebooks, and service-specific interfaces, which can complicate reproducibility, inspection, and communication of runs [2, 4]. This fragmentation makes it difficult to preserve intermediate artifacts across partial reruns, use heterogeneous compute backends, or separate scientific interpretation from the execution environment [6, 12].

In this paper, we present `protein_pipeline`, a system for orchestrating protein design workflows as a unified, reproducible, and interactive research process rather than as a collection of loosely connected tools. The platform integrates staged execution across external modeling services with a web-based console for run setup, monitoring, comparison, and report generation, while maintaining structured artifact storage under a run-centric organization. The central contribution is therefore systems-oriented: `protein_pipeline` operationalizes workflow control through an MCP-enabled backend that forms a tight feedback loop with an integrated multi-agent panel. This architecture allows the system to autonomously interpret scientific quality signals—such as structural confidence and sequence conservation—to automatically guide researchers away from failure modes, providing checkpoint-aware execution and artifact traceability in a single platform that supports both routine experimentation and comparative study design [13, 17].

By framing protein design as a workflow orchestration problem, `protein_pipeline` addresses a practical gap between powerful individual models and usable research infrastructure. The system is designed to make complex design campaigns easier to launch, safer to rerun, simpler to audit, and more efficient to analyze, thereby supporting reproducible AI-for-science practice in computational protein engineering.

The closest conceptual neighbors are integrated AI-for-science platforms that treat execution, provenance, and interpretation as first-class objects, but `protein_pipeline` specializes this idea for protein design by coupling external modeling services with a Model Context Protocol (MCP) layer for agentic tool use and automated reporting [4, 13].

Accordingly, the paper should be read as a systems and platform contribution: it addresses the operational gap between powerful but fragmented protein modeling tools and the practical needs of researchers who must run, inspect, reproduce, and communicate multi-stage design campaigns.

## Problem Statement and Design Goals

Computational protein design workflows typically require chaining together heterogeneous tools for sequence retrieval, MSA construction, backbone generation or selection, sequence design, filtering, structure prediction, and downstream comparison. In many research settings, these stages are coordinated through ad hoc scripts and model-specific interfaces, which makes execution brittle, obscures provenance, and complicates partial reruns when upstream inputs or parameters change [8, 32]. A second limitation is that analysis and interpretation are often separated from execution: intermediate artifacts are scattered across filesystems, comparisons are performed manually, and report generation occurs outside the workflow itself, reducing reproducibility and slowing iteration [27, 30]. Existing web front ends for scientific pipelines also frequently act only as job submission layers, without exposing stage-aware state, artifact lineage, or integrated comparative views needed for research use [28, 29].

`protein_pipeline` is designed to address these workflow-level gaps rather than to introduce a new standalone prediction model. The system is guided by five design goals: (1) unified orchestration across heterogeneous external services and execution environments; (2) stage-aware, checkpointed execution with safe rerun semantics; (3) structured artifact management under a stable `run_id` to preserve requests, intermediate outputs, status history, and summaries; (4) an interactive web interface that supports setup, monitoring, comparison, and report generation as first-class research activities; and (5) extensibility through an MCP-enabled tool layer, so that additional modeling or scoring stages can be incorporated without redesigning the full platform [4, 13, 22]. Framed this way, the contribution of `protein_pipeline` is a reproducible and inspectable systems layer that turns a fragmented protein design procedure into an operational research environment.

## System Overview

`protein_pipeline` is designed as a unified research system for computational protein design, integrating workflow execution, artifact management, interactive inspection, and comparative reporting within a single platform. Rather than introducing a new standalone prediction model, the system addresses the practical systems problem that modern protein design studies are typically distributed across heterogeneous tools, infrastructure environments, and manual analysis steps, which complicates reproducibility, reruns, and interpretation [8, 32]. The platform therefore treats a design run as a first-class, traceable object: user inputs, stage configurations, intermediate outputs, status events, summaries, and reports are all organized under a persistent `run_id` and exposed through both programmatic and web-based interfaces [4, 11].

At a high level, the system connects five layers: a static web console for setup and monitoring; an MCP/HTTP backend exposing execution and analysis tools; a pipeline runner that enforces stage order and rerun semantics; external modeling and scoring services such as MMseqs2, ProteinMPNN, SoluProt, and AlphaFold2; and a structured artifact store rooted at `PIPELINE_OUTPUT_ROOT/<run_id>/`. This architecture decouples user interaction from model-specific runtime details while preserving end-to-end provenance across the workflow. In practice, users can configure a run, launch or resume staged execution, inspect intermediate artifacts, compare outputs across runs, and generate exportable summaries with automated agentic insights through the integrated agent panel [13, 17, 28].

## Architecture

`protein_pipeline` is organized as a modular, stage-oriented system that couples workflow orchestration, artifact management, and interactive analysis within a single research platform. At a high level, the architecture separates concerns between (i) experiment specification and execution control, (ii) computational back-end stages for sequence generation, structure prediction, scoring, and downstream analysis, and (iii) a web-based interface for configuration, monitoring, comparison, and report generation. This separation allows individual components to evolve independently while preserving a consistent end-to-end user workflow and reproducible execution record [13, 27, 32].

The execution layer is built around explicit pipeline stages with well-defined inputs, outputs, and metadata, enabling chained processing across heterogeneous protein design tools. A central component is the ToolDispatcher, which functions as the MCP-enabled execution layer. By exposing modeling and analysis functions through a unified protocol, the ToolDispatcher allows the multi-agent panel to programmatically query stage status, retrieve intermediate artifacts, and even re-invoke modeling services with modified parameters. This design enables a dynamic feedback loop where agentic interpretation directly informs subsequent tool calls, moving beyond static job scheduling. Each stage emits versioned artifacts and structured logs—including `status.json` and append-only event streams—that can be consumed by subsequent stages or surfaced directly in the user interface. This supports both full end-to-end runs and partial re-execution of selected stages, which is essential for iterative design studies where users may wish to modify prompts, model settings, or scoring criteria without rebuilding the entire workflow from scratch [4, 30].

Above this execution layer, a multi-agent consensus panel monitors the "health" of each stage by analyzing domain-specific metrics. For example, a structure agent might evaluate AlphaFold2 pLDDT and RMSD values, while a protein agent assesses MSA depth and conservation signals. The system then builds a consensus decision—"proceed", "monitor", or "recover" — to automate quality control across iterative rounds of design [13, 17].

## Workflow Execution Model

`protein_pipeline` implements a stage-aware execution model designed to make heterogeneous protein design workflows reproducible, restartable, and inspectable. Rather than treating the workflow as a single opaque job, the system represents execution as an ordered sequence of named stages with explicit inputs, outputs, and persisted state under a unique `run_id`. In the current configuration, the canonical path is `msa -> rfd3 -> bioemu -> design -> soluprot -> af2 -> novelty`, with auxiliary artifacts for conservation analysis, masking, wild-type comparison, summaries, and report generation. This decomposition reflects common multi-tool protein engineering practice while reducing the operational fragility of ad hoc script chaining [26, 32].

Execution begins with a structured request submitted through the web console or MCP/HTTP interface. A preflight step validates request completeness and service availability before compute-intensive stages are launched. The pipeline runner then resolves stage dependencies, assigns or reuses a `run_id`, and dispatches individual tasks to external services such as MMseqs2, ProteinMPNN, SoluProt, and AlphaFold2-compatible endpoints. Each stage writes machine-readable artifacts, status updates, and event logs to the run directory, allowing downstream stages, the user interface, and reporting tools to consume a consistent record of execution without relying on transient process state [4, 13].

A key design feature is safe partial rerun behavior. Because intermediate artifacts are checkpointed by stage, users can inspect prior outputs, resume from later stages when upstream inputs are unchanged, or selectively re-execute portions of the workflow when parameters are modified. The multi-agent panel provides automated intervention during these reruns by monitoring concrete quality thresholds. For instance, an MSA depth below 50 sequences triggers a "recover" recommendation to increase the `mmseqs_max_seqs` parameter or switch target databases. Similarly, a SoluProt solubility pass rate below 20% or an average AlphaFold2 pLDDT score below 75 will trigger a "monitor" status, with the consensus mechanism suggesting recovery actions such as adjusting ProteinMPNN sampling temperatures or relaxing structural constraints. This model supports iterative experimental use while preserving provenance: requests, stage outputs, summaries, and reports remain linked within the same run-scoped artifact structure [17, 33].

This execution model also supports the system’s interactive analysis goals. Since every stage emits standardized artifacts and timeline information, the web console can expose monitoring, comparison, and report-generation functions directly on top of the same execution record used by the backend orchestrator. The workflow therefore serves not only as a compute pipeline but as the backbone of a research environment in which design generation, structure evaluation, and comparative review are integrated into a single reproducible system [28, 29].

## Artifact Model and Reproducibility

A central design goal of `protein_pipeline` is to make each workflow run reproducible, inspectable, and safely reusable. To support this, the system adopts a run-centric artifact model in which every execution is assigned a persistent `run_id` and all inputs, intermediate products, status records, and derived reports are written under a structured directory rooted at `PIPELINE_OUTPUT_ROOT/<run_id>/`. This organization treats workflow artifacts as explicit system outputs that can support later inspection and reporting [30, 32].

For each run, the artifact store preserves the original `request.json` specification, stage outputs (e.g., `msa.a3m`, `design.fasta`, `af2_relaxed.pdb`), and execution metadata such as `status.json` and append-only event logs (`events.jsonl`). This design provides a stable provenance trail from user-submitted inputs to final candidate rankings, while also exposing enough intermediate state to diagnose failures and audit parameter choices. By using a shared artifact schema across execution and analysis APIs, the system supports web-console features such as reconstructing run history and previewing structural outputs without relying on transient in-memory state [4, 11].

The artifact model also supports stage-aware rerun semantics. Rather than treating a workflow as a monolithic job, `protein_pipeline` records outputs at stage boundaries so that completed steps can be reused when appropriate and invalidated when upstream inputs or configuration change. This reduces operational risk during partial reruns, where silent reuse of stale intermediates is a common source of irreproducibility. By coupling checkpointed execution with explicit run records, the system makes it easier to distinguish fresh computation from reused artifacts and to preserve a traceable lineage across iterative design cycles [13, 33].

More broadly, this artifact-centered approach connects reproducibility with usability. The same stored objects that enable provenance and rerun safety also power downstream comparison, hit-list generation, export packaging, and report authoring. In this sense, reproducibility is not implemented as a separate archival layer added after computation; it is embedded directly into the operational model of the platform [26, 28].

## Web Console and User Workflow

A distinguishing feature of `protein_pipeline` is that the web console is treated as part of the scientific system rather than as a thin submission front end. The interface supports the full user workflow: configuring inputs such as FASTA, PDB, ligand, and stage-specific controls; running preflight validation via `pipeline.preflight`; launching or resuming staged executions; monitoring status and checkpoints; inspecting intermediate artifacts; comparing completed runs; and exporting summaries and reports for downstream communication [15, 28]. This design reflects the project’s central premise that execution, interpretation, and reporting should be integrated into a single research environment.

In practice, the console interacts with the MCP/HTTP backend through structured operations such as run setup, status queries, artifact listing, comparison, and report generation. Because all outputs are organized under a persistent `run_id`, users can revisit prior runs, inspect stage-level provenance, and safely perform partial reruns without losing the context needed to interpret downstream results [29, 31]. The result is a workflow in which operational state and scientific outputs remain tightly coupled: users can move from job configuration to comparative analysis within the same interface, reducing reliance on ad hoc scripts and manual file handling [11, 20].

## Implementation Details

`protein_pipeline` is implemented as a layered research system that separates user interaction, workflow orchestration, external model execution, and artifact persistence while keeping these components connected through a shared run-centric data model. A static web console provides interfaces for run setup, preflight validation, execution control, monitoring, comparison, and report export. These actions are routed to an MCP/HTTP backend that exposes tool-like operations such as run submission, status inspection, artifact listing, and comparative analysis, allowing the same system surface to support both interactive use and programmatic automation [4, 13, 23].

At execution time, the pipeline runner validates inputs, assigns or reuses a `run_id`, resolves stage dependencies, and executes the canonical workflow `msa -> rfd3 -> bioemu -> design -> soluprot -> af2 -> novelty`, with optional branches for backbone generation, masking, conservation analysis, and reporting. Heavy-compute stages are delegated to external services, including MMseqs2 for MSA generation, ProteinMPNN for sequence design, SoluProt for filtering, and AF2-style structure evaluation, with optional RFD3 and BioEmu stages when backbone generation is required. This design decouples orchestration from model-specific runtimes and infrastructure assumptions, making it easier to swap services or rerun selected stages without rewriting the surrounding workflow logic [12, 22].

A central implementation feature is the structured artifact store under `PIPELINE_OUTPUT_ROOT/<run_id>/`, where the system persists `request.json`, `status.json`, `events.jsonl`, stage outputs, summaries, and generated reports. This layout makes intermediate products first-class objects rather than transient files, enabling checkpointed execution, safe partial reruns, and post hoc inspection through the web console. Because comparison and reporting operate directly over stored run artifacts, downstream interpretation is integrated into the same system that produced the results, improving reproducibility and reducing the manual bookkeeping typical of ad hoc protein design pipelines [8, 32].

## System Evaluation

We evaluate `protein_pipeline` as a research systems contribution rather than as a single predictive model. Accordingly, the assessment focuses on whether the platform reliably orchestrates multi-stage protein design workflows, improves reproducibility of computational experiments, and supports practical inspection and comparison of generated candidates across sequence generation, structure prediction, scoring, and downstream analysis stages [13, 17]. The central evaluation question is whether integrating these components into one MCP-enabled, web-accessible system reduces operational friction relative to ad hoc script collections while preserving flexibility for iterative scientific use [12].

Our evaluation framework therefore emphasizes system-level properties: end-to-end execution coverage, artifact traceability, parameterized reruns, monitoring of intermediate states, and usability of comparative analysis outputs. In this framing, a successful run is not only one that produces candidate proteins, but one that also records configuration choices, links outputs across stages, and enables researchers to revisit, compare, and report results without reconstructing provenance manually [13]. This is especially important in protein design settings, where experimental conclusions often depend on ranked subsets, filtering thresholds, and cross-stage consistency rather than on a single terminal score.

We further assess the practical value of the web console and reporting layer as part of the system contribution. The interface is intended to support setup, execution tracking, candidate review, and exportable summaries within the same environment, thereby shortening the loop between computation and interpretation. From a systems perspective, this interactive layer is important because it turns pipeline outputs into inspectable research artifacts and makes comparative evaluation across runs more accessible to both method developers and domain scientists [11, 28].

## Representative Use Cases

Representative use cases for `protein_pipeline` center on workflow orchestration, reproducible evaluation, and interactive scientific review rather than on any single predictive model. A first use case is end-to-end execution of a standard protein design campaign in which a researcher starts from a target sequence or structure, configures optional backbone-generation and masking settings, launches the staged workflow, and receives a run-scoped collection of design candidates, structure predictions, filtering outputs, and summary artifacts. In this setting, the system reduces the operational burden of coordinating heterogeneous tools while preserving stage order, intermediate outputs, and rerun safety [15].

A second use case is comparative analysis across multiple design runs. Because outputs are organized under stable `run_id` directories and exposed through comparison and reporting interfaces, users can inspect how changes in inputs, fixed-position strategies, solubility thresholds, or structure-evaluation settings affect downstream candidate quality. This supports practical experimental iteration, where the key task is not merely launching jobs but understanding why one configuration produced more promising candidates than another [16].

A third use case is research communication and handoff. The same system that executes the workflow also stores requests, status timelines, stage artifacts, summaries, and report-ready outputs, making it easier to package results for collaborators or for later reanalysis. This is especially valuable in protein design settings where conclusions often depend on tracing candidate provenance across multiple computational stages and external services. By integrating execution, inspection, and export in one environment, `protein_pipeline` functions as a reusable research platform for computational protein engineering teams [17].

## Discussion

`protein_pipeline` is best understood as a systems contribution that addresses a practical bottleneck in computational protein design: although sequence generation, structure prediction, scoring, and comparative analysis are individually well supported by existing tools, researchers often still rely on fragmented scripts, ad hoc file handling, and manual result collation to connect these stages into a usable end-to-end workflow. By integrating these components into a single MCP-enabled pipeline with a web-based control and analysis layer, the platform shifts effort away from orchestration overhead and toward experimental design, interpretation, and iteration [13, 23].

A central implication of this work is that workflow design itself can materially affect research productivity and reproducibility. In protein design settings, small differences in configuration, model versions, intermediate filtering, or artifact naming can make runs difficult to compare or reproduce after the fact. `protein_pipeline` addresses this by treating execution metadata, generated artifacts, and downstream reports as first-class outputs of the system rather than incidental byproducts. This design supports more transparent comparison across runs, easier handoff between collaborators, and more reliable retrospective analysis, which are increasingly important as design campaigns become larger and more heterogeneous [18, 32].

The web-integrated interface is also significant from a usability perspective. Many powerful scientific pipelines remain accessible primarily to expert users comfortable with command-line tooling and custom environment management. By exposing setup, monitoring, comparison, and reporting through an interactive console, `protein_pipeline` lowers operational barriers while preserving the structure needed for rigorous experimentation. This does not replace expert control; rather, it provides a more legible and inspectable layer over complex multi-stage execution, which may support use by interdisciplinary teams that include computational biologists, protein engineers, and software developers [20, 29].

At the same time, the present work should be interpreted as infrastructure rather than as a claim of a new state-of-the-art design model. Its value depends on the quality and suitability of the underlying generation, prediction, and scoring components, and future improvements in those modules can be incorporated without changing the core system framing. Important next steps include broader benchmarking in real laboratory workflows, tighter support for extensible model backends and analysis plugins, and deeper integration with collaborative research and provenance standards. In that sense, `protein_pipeline` provides a foundation for reproducible, interactive protein design research, while leaving substantial room for community-driven expansion and empirical validation in diverse use cases [22, 26].

## Limitations

Although `protein_pipeline` improves orchestration, traceability, and usability for multi-stage protein design, it does not remove the scientific and operational limitations of the underlying tools. End-to-end output quality remains bounded by the accuracy, calibration, and failure modes of external components such as MSA generation, sequence design, solubility prediction, structure prediction, and novelty assessment; errors or biases introduced early in the workflow can propagate downstream despite checkpointed capture [30, 32]. The platform also depends on heterogeneous remote services and compute backends, so runtime, cost, availability, and version drift can affect reproducibility unless deployments are carefully pinned and monitored [12, 34]. In addition, the current system contribution is primarily infrastructural rather than algorithmic: the platform standardizes execution and analysis, but it does not by itself establish that any particular pipeline configuration yields superior biological outcomes across targets or experimental settings [1]. Finally, while the web console and report generation improve accessibility, effective use still requires domain expertise to choose inputs, interpret ranking signals, and decide which candidates merit experimental follow-up; automated summaries should therefore be treated as decision support rather than as substitutes for expert review or wet-lab validation [11, 28].

## Future Work

Several extensions would strengthen `protein_pipeline` as a general research platform. First, the orchestration layer could be expanded to support a broader set of interchangeable design and evaluation modules, enabling users to swap sequence generators, structure predictors, and scoring functions without changing the surrounding execution and artifact model [13, 26]. Second, tighter provenance capture and workflow versioning could improve long-term reproducibility by recording model revisions, service configurations, environment metadata, and stage-level dependency hashes alongside each `run_id` [32, 34]. Third, the web console could evolve from run monitoring toward more interactive scientific decision support, including richer candidate triage, cross-run analytics, and collaborative annotation workflows for team-based protein engineering projects [29, 31]. Finally, future validation should include broader deployment studies across diverse protein design tasks and user groups to quantify usability, robustness, and the practical impact of integrated workflow systems on research throughput and interpretability [11, 28].

## Conclusion

`protein_pipeline` demonstrates that a substantial contribution to computational protein design can come from workflow integration rather than from a new standalone predictive model. By combining staged orchestration, MCP/HTTP-accessible execution services, structured artifact management under `run_id`, and a web console for setup, monitoring, comparison, and report generation, the system turns a fragmented sequence of design and evaluation tasks into a reproducible research environment. This architecture is intended to reduce operational overhead, improve traceability of intermediate results, and make downstream interpretation and communication part of the same system that executes the workflow [13, 17, 32].

More broadly, the platform suggests a practical systems pattern for AI-for-science applications in which heterogeneous external tools are coordinated through stage-aware execution, safe rerun semantics, and persistent machine-readable outputs. In the context of protein engineering, this enables users to move more efficiently from input specification to candidate generation, structural evaluation, novelty assessment, and comparative review without relying on ad hoc scripts or disconnected analysis steps [4, 13, 26].

Future work includes expanding support for additional modeling backends, strengthening provenance and validation features, and conducting broader user-centered evaluations of usability, reproducibility, and scientific throughput across diverse protein design tasks. We expect that systems of this kind will become increasingly important as protein design workflows continue to grow in complexity and as researchers demand interfaces that support both execution and scientific reasoning within a single platform [23, 29].
